Analyst Commentary

Vixio strategic intelligence · payments resilience theme
Q2 2026 EDITION

New Operational Resilience frameworks are being built across the world, with the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) taking effect in the EU. Understanding where regulators are intervening — and which triggers are repeatable across jurisdictions — is now table-stakes for any cross-border programme. With DORA still maturing, the number of enforcement actions remains a leading rather than a lagging indicator, but the trend across the world stage is unmistakable.

Globally there has been a sharp shift towards third-party scrutiny, with vendor oversight being the recurring weakness in firms that have been penalised. The data collected shows clearly that nations differ in how they enforce, even when they converge on what they enforce. The 2025 dataset spans seven jurisdictions with functional operational resilience frameworks: the US, the UK, Peru, Hong Kong, Australia, the UAE, and South Africa.

In the United States, the severity score reaches 8.2, driven by total cumulative fines of $15,000,000 USD and a high tempo of enforcement actions across fraud protection and third-party oversight. Compliance teams operating in the US should treat internal controls and supply-chain mapping as a continuous discipline, especially where client-facing software is in the critical path.

The UK faces similar barriers but with a different posture: it posts the highest mean severity score globally on only three actions, an 8.3 average. That reflects the FCA and Bank of England's preference for a small number of high-impact, precedent-setting interventions — particularly around third-party oversight failures and IT governance. Programmes targeting the UK should expect fewer but heavier interventions, and plan governance evidence accordingly.

Across APAC, regulators are communicating actively about the issues organisations face. Hong Kong sits at 7.75, with triggers concentrated in cloud resilience, monitoring logic, database latency and concurrent-load failures — all infrastructure-level risks that translate directly into platform and vendor obligations. Australia's regulators (ASIC, APRA, RBA) are increasingly issuing non-monetary but structurally significant interventions — capital add-ons, regulatory directions, court orders — signalling that capital and licence instruments are increasingly preferred over fines for systemic infrastructure. Australia sits at 6, a middle ground between the high-enforcement US/UK envelope and lower-intensity markets such as South Africa.

Compared with MENA and the single LATAM datapoint (Peru), the patterns rhyme even where intensity differs. Technology triggers — API stability, batch-processing delay, data-sync error — recur across mature and emerging markets alike. The UAE sits at 7, South Africa and Peru at 5. That convergence on what regulators care about means a single resilience framework can be tuned across MENA, LATAM and APAC; the calibration that needs to vary is severity, not substance. Programmes that stand up one model and adapt enforcement intensity by jurisdiction avoid duplicating policy work.

Strategic implication. Use this data to anticipate disruptions and to monitor how different markets enforce against breaches. With the US and UK leading enforcement intensity, regulated firms should expect heavier preparation costs there. Globally, however, the pattern is consistent: enforcement clusters around fraud controls, cybersecurity, and above all third-party oversight.

Analyst Commentary

Vixio strategic intelligence · payment ecosystem & digital adoption
Q2 2026 EDITION

The regulatory landscape now sits on top of an increasingly digital payments stack. Pix in Brazil, UPI in India, SEPA within the EU, the global card networks, and a long tail of domestic real-time schemes — each carries its own licensing, conduct, and resilience obligations. The 2024 data shows an exponential rise in digital payment methods across the globe; UPI in India and Pix in Brazil are the clearest case studies of what regulator-led rails do to a market.

Looked at as mobile money, India now hosts a dense set of wallets all interoperating through UPI — Unified Payments Interface. UPI has grown exponentially: 31.8% of the South Asian population transacts on UPI. Cash usage in consumer spending fell from 80% in 2021 to 60% in 2024, with ATM withdrawals as the leading indicator. Any payments programme entering India without a UPI strategy is unlikely to gain meaningful share — and supervisory expectations are increasingly UPI-shaped, especially around outage reporting and reconciliation.

In Brazil, Pix has become the dominant payment form: as of 2025, 78.4% of Brazilians use Pix, compared with 68% using cash. The Brazilian central bank has actively widened Pix's scope since its 2020 launch, making it one of the most widely adopted payment mechanisms in the world. As card rails are gradually de-prioritised, the regulatory questions for institutions operating in Brazil shift accordingly — fraud monitoring, dispute handling, and consumer-protection obligations are all being re-shaped around Pix.

Across emerging markets, government-run digital rails increasingly displace the card-only model. The strategic implication is two-fold: cross-border reach improves, and so does the dependency on UPI, Pix, and the African RTP networks — each with its own resilience and conduct expectations. Firms that map their compliance programmes to these rails — rather than assuming card-style oversight — position themselves to operate confidently in the Global South, where digital payment schemes are doing the most to formalise economic activity.

For B2B and enterprise platforms, the implication is the same: as small businesses in emerging markets expect real-time bank transfers instead of corporate credit lines, and as biometric-authenticated one-click checkouts become the norm, the regulatory load that comes with each rail must travel with the deployment. The Vixio Live Intelligence Console is built so this load is visible before the deployment ships, not after the regulator notices.
